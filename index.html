The Universe is a Beautiful Orchestra of Cycles...

Silently turning, these cycles create order out of the seeming chaos that is space.

We can see some of them in our everyday lives, others we experience but may not be aware of, and even others seem unnoticeable.

Water, carbon, and nitrogen cycle through Earth’s landscapes.

Business cycles, school cycles, sleep cycles, relationship cycles, on and on, all around us. It’s crazy.

Now, step back.

Our planet rotates around its own axis. Our moon, Luna, cycles around us down here on Earth. The Earth, and our seven other planet brethren [sorry, Pluto], cycle around the Sun. And our solar system, along with the billions of others in our galaxy, cycle around the Milky Way Galaxy like ants on a giant pinwheel.

Cycles on cycles on cycles.

That’s a lot to wrap your head around. Well yeah, it’s outerspace. Let’s crunch it down into something more bitesize and see how you fit into all of this.

Cycles of the Earth
on its Axis

Every day, our planet completes one full rotation around its axis.

If you’re on the beach near the equator, you’d be moving close to 1000 miles per hour and traveling nearly 24,900 miles each day.

Thanks to gravity, we move with the Earth and don’t notice its rotation in everyday life.

Lunar cycles

Our moon, scientists call her Luna, makes her full orbit around the planet about every 27 days.

Fine, every 27 days, 7 hours, 43 minutes, and 11.6 seconds.

Because Luna spins at the same speed she orbits Earth, and opposite to the Earth’s rotation, we have always seen the same face of the moon. Talk about a one-sided personality.

Cycles of the Intl.
Space Station

Traveling a whopping 5 miles per second, the International Space Station orbits the Earth every 90 minutes.

You can’t go here for your birthday party, but you can sign up for a text or email alert telling you when, and where, you can spot it moving across the sky. [You won’t even need a telescope!]

Solar Eclipses

Syzygy.
What?
Syzygy, it’s when the Sun and Moon align from the Earth’s perspective. In the case of a solar eclipse, the Moon passes between the Earth and Sun.

There can be at least 2, and up to 5, solar eclipses each year. No more than 2 can be total eclipses though, sorry.

Cycles of the Earth
Around the Sun

It takes one year, or 365 days if you fancy, for the Earth and its lovely little inhabitants to orbit around the sun. [Don’t worry, you’re tall, just not in the scheme of solar systems.]

In order to take its close to 600 million mile orbital path in a year, the Earth travels 66,000 miles per hour. Never hitting the breaks.
If you see the sun, don’t look into it, but say thanks.

Years on...

If you don’t want to grow up, going to a different planet will change your numeric age but won’t help with any of the hard stuff.

One year is measured by the time it takes that planet to orbit the Sun. Because of the Sun’s gravitational pull, planets closer to the Sun move faster and have shorter years than planets further away. [Insert age joke here]

Galactic Cycles

Speaking of cycles, the entire Milky Way Galaxy is spinning. Think big pinwheel.

It takes the Sun about 225 million years to go around the galaxy once, no small feat. In galactic years, the Sun and earth are about 20, almost of legal drinking age. Yet, in all of recorded human history, we’ve hardly moved in our cycle around the galaxy.

The universe has been around a long time and isn't going anywhere, well, it is; it's always expanding. But you get the point.
